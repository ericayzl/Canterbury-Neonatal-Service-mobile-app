Our Team

Canterbury Neonatal Service has a multi-disciplinary team to contribute to your baby's health. This page describes the different staff involved in caring for your baby.

If you want to contact us, scroll down the page to our phone number, where you can make general inquiries and be directed to specific staff members if necessary.

ACNM: Associate Clinical Nurse Manager

Provides clinical coordination and professional leadership, support, guidance, and coaching to the nursing staff of the neonatal service on a shift-by-shift basis, ensuring safe, effective, quality care.

Clinical Director

Provides leadership for the service in conjunction with the Nurse Manager. The CD is responsible for coordinating the team, delivering the medical management of the babies in our service, and representing the service nationally and internationally.

Neonatal Nurse Manager

The nurse manager coordinates the nursing and support team across the neonatal inpatient and outreach services, facilitating and promoting excellence in patient care.

Safety and Quality Unit

The Women's and Children's Safety and Quality Unit (SQU) oversees the quality and safety of our services and provides guidance, tools, and advice to clinical/operational staff and managers. We are continuously monitoring the quality of care the users of our services receive and looking for ways to improve this collectively.

Clinical Nurse Specialist (Advanced Neonatal Practitioner), CNS (ANP)

Clinical Nurse Specialists (CNS), or Advanced Neonatal Practitioners (ANPs), are senior nurses who have many years of experience and have done specialty training to work on the medical team. They rotate around different areas of the Neonatal service, with the registrars covering different areas of the neonatal, postnatal, delivery suite, and retrievals. They are responsible for the day-to-day medical management of your infants and will discuss any concerns with the Neonatal consultant on duty.

Enrolled Nurse

Provides quality nursing care, support, and education to babies and their parents within the Neonatal Service's Special Care/Level 2 environment.

Infection Control and Prevention Nurse Specialist

The Clinical Nurse Specialist in Infection Prevention and Control (IP&C) is an information/advisory resource with specialist education in this nursing field. This specialist nurse can provide education and support concerning any IP&C issues to parents and staff in the neonatal unit e.g., infectious diseases, and how we control these in the unit. In addition, printed resources are available, which may also be helpful for parents.

Karitane Nurse

Provides a strong parent craft model of nursing care to babies and their parents within the Special Care/Level 2 environment. This includes consistent and current advice on lactation and discharge preparation.

Neonatal Outreach Nurses

A team of neonatal nurses who follow up on a selection of babies from the neonatal unit after discharge to provide support and advice.

Registered Nurse

Provides a high level of clinical nursing care for neonates within the neonatal service and their families.

Pediatricians

Consultant Neonatal Paediatricians: the pediatricians are responsible for the overall medical management of your baby. We have six neonatal consultants who rotate through service in the neonatal unit. You will see a lot more of the consultant who is "on service" for that week. If you would like to speak to your consultant, please indicate to your nurse or the ACNM on duty. They will also follow your baby up after discharge if indicated.

Radiologists

We are a team of five Radiologists (located at Christchurch Women's Hospital and Christchurch Public Hospital) who specialises in Neonatal and Obstetric Radiology. We assess and diagnose neonatal abnormalities on several different forms of imaging. This most commonly includes neonatal X-rays and ultrasounds but may include CT, MRI, and fluoroscopy. We use a state-of-the-art system called PACS which displays the images electronically and allows rapid communication of the results with the Neonatologists. We also discuss the cases at a weekly multidisciplinary X-ray meeting with the Paediatric surgeons and Neonatologists.

Registrars

Neonatal registrar: registrars are pediatricians-in-training (with several years of practice as doctors already). They rotate around different areas of the Neonatal service with the CNS (ANP) covering different areas of the neonatal, postnatal, delivery suite, and retrievals. They are responsible for the day-to-day medical management of your infants. They will discuss any concerns with the Neonatal consultant on duty.

Infant Feeding Specialist (Clinical Nurse Specialist)

The Clinical Nurse Specialist supports staff and families in breastfeeding education and clinical matters.

Associate Clinical Nurse Manager – Breastfeeding Initiative

Monitors Breastfeeding Initiative standards around the ten steps to successful breastfeeding, the non-breastfeeding mother, the International Code of Marketing and subsequent resolution, and the Treaty of Waitangi.

Feeding Team

Clinical expertise in breastfeeding and supports colleagues in clinical breastfeeding skills.

Neurodevelopmental Physiotherapist

Assesses complex babies for neurodevelopmental feeding, sucking, and swallowing issues.

Speech Language Therapist

Assesses the suck and swallow of complex babies and implements a program involving staff and parents.

Discharge Planner

A nurse who works with complex babies plays a vital role in setting up specifically targeted programs and recourses for discharge into the community, e.g., teaching home feeding via a pump.

Discharge Facilitator

The role of the discharge facilitator is to assist in the preparation of families transitioning to home. In particular, families with ongoing medical needs, such as home oxygen and tube feeding. Discharge facilitators are part of a multidisciplinary team caring for the family and liaise closely with the Neonatal Outreach Nurses. Contact details are 364 ​​4244 or pager numbers 5291 / 5487.

Māori Health Worker

Part of the multidisciplinary team, this role is to tautoko (support) and awhi (help) parents on their journey in NICU and provide a culturally safe environment.

Social Work Team

There are three Social Workers in NICU, providing 80 hours of support each week. We assist with practical and emotional issues that may arise. We aim to work alongside families to help reduce stress so parents can attach and experience the most positive start possible with their baby/babies. We can refer families to agencies when they require support following discharge.

NICU Social Workers work with families providing emotional and practical support. We understand the complexity of the issues in the NICU. Our role is to walk beside families through the NICU journey to help reduce stress in the unit and at home.

Dietitian

The Neonatal Dietitian is here to ensure your baby receives the best nutrition and will be monitoring your baby's growth and nutritional intake. She is on the morning ward rounds to look at growth charts and to discuss and implement feeding changes. If you have questions regarding what nutrition your baby is receiving or your baby's growth, then ask your nurse to contact the Dietitian, who can come and discuss this with you. The Dietitian can also advise breastfeeding mothers on their own nutrition, which can be an issue for many busy parents. She is available Monday to Friday.

Hearing Screening Team

We offer hearing screening to all babies admitted to NICU as part of the national Universal Newborn Hearing Screening and Early Intervention Programme. At some point during a baby's NICU journey, parents/caregivers are given written information about the program. We have a team of four NICU nurses who complete the screening of the babies admitted to the NICU.

As a baby nears discharge home (usually after 36 weeks), one of the nurses on the hearing screening team will approach parents/caregivers to see if they would be interested in having their baby screened. It is an excellent opportunity for parents/caregivers to ask questions they may have about the screening. Hearing screening is a simple process; our team will give parents/caregivers the screening results immediately.

Pharmacist

A Clinical Pharmacist visits the Neonatal Unit daily (Monday - Friday). The visit will usually include attending the ward round and providing medication dosing advice to the medical team and any parents with medication-related queries about their infant's care. As part of the ward visit, the pharmacist will also check medication charts to ensure that all prescriptions have been written correctly and that the medicines can be given safely and on time.

Nearer to the time of discharge home, if your infant needs to be continue medicines at home, the pharmacist will help to ensure that this can happen as easily as possible. In some cases, discharge medicines may need to be dispensed from the hospital pharmacy rather than your local community pharmacy after discharge. The Neonatal Clinical Pharmacist and Discharge Facilitator can advise you if this is the case. During your infant's stay in the NICU, please ask the pharmacist if you have any questions about the medication you think they may be able to help with.

Physiotherapist

Physiotherapists assess infants' development, head shape, range of movement, and muscle strength, meet with parents for specific handling and positioning sessions, discuss developmental play ideas, and liaise with community therapists if infants have ongoing needs. Physiotherapists also run a developmental playgroup for preterm infants to ensure the achievement of developmental milestones in the first year of life.

Speech Language Therapy

A Speech Language Therapy service is available to all babies admitted to the NICU. We offer a Monday-to-Friday service, and three therapists are currently on the unit. Speech Language Therapists assess your baby's sucking and feeding skills and will create individual feeding plans to help with his or her sucking and feeding. For those babies that are bottle-fed, we may recommend specialist teats.

We also work with the infant feeding specialist, the physiotherapist, and the medical team to advise feeding positions and routines. We usually see babies born early, those with syndromes, cleft palate, or any baby having difficulty feeding. Babies can have feeding difficulties for various reasons, so our involvement may be for a short or long time. For babies in the NICU for a long time, we also advise about early communication development. Outpatient follow-up is also available once your baby has gone home.

Hospital Aide

To facilitate appropriate cleaning and setting up of equipment to support the Clinical Team in caring for the neonate and to ensure adequate stock levels are maintained.

Medical Technicians

Clinical Engineering takes care of the medical equipment and systems. This includes advice on purchasing, operating, and maintaining medical equipment. The development of medical equipment systems to meet the clinical requirements is also included.

Milk Room Aide

To facilitate the appropriate making of milk feeds and milk additives for infants within the Neonatal Services.

The Christchurch Women's Neonatal Transport Team covers retrievals of newborn, premature, and sick babies from Kaikoura to the West Coast and down to Timaru, as not all hospitals have the resources to care for preterm or sick babies. The team also covers retrieving babies requiring surgical intervention from Nelson to Invercargill and taking them to Christchurch Women's Hospital, the only hospital that provides surgical services to neonates in the South Island.

​​​​​The transport team may also collect preterm or sick babies from other hospitals around New Zealand if the retrieval team from the referring hospital cannot do the transport. The transport team will also transfer babies back to their home hospitals once they are well enough and are almost ready for discharge home.

The Neonatal Emergency Transport Team aims to provide the same specialised care to newborn, premature, and sick babies as they would receive within the Neonatal Intensive Care Unit environment to reduce morbidity and mortality. The team is on call 24 hours a day, seven days a week.

The transport team comprises a dedicated team of specially trained Registered Nurses/Midwives, a Clinical Nurse Specialist/Advanced Nurse Practitioner, and a Neonatal Registrar.

Our service transports the babies by Road Ambulance (St John), Fixed Wing Aircraft (New Zealand Flying Doctors), or Helicopter (Garden City Helicopter).

The Neonatal Transport Team does between 60-80 emergency retrievals a year and 40 transfers of babies back to their home hospital. About half of the transports are by ambulance, half by fixed wing, and only a few by helicopter.
